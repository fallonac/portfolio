About me

Currently, I am a first-year student pursuing my Bachelor of Architecture at Carnegie Mellon University. I call the Chicagoland area my home, where I grew up fascinated by the difference in architectural styles between the suburbs and vastly urban contexts of the cityscape. I am very interested in architectural internships to build a foundation as a young professional, to further gain experience both digitally and in analog, and to develop professional communication skills.

Thank you for visiting my site! Please do not hesitate to connect with me either via email or social media!

Motion

Spring 2017

A household object is designed to perform its necessary functions with ease of use, and upon closer inspection, the way in which lines in elevation interact with each other demonstrate this. My intense study of a cheese grater illustrated these relationships through the use of drafting necessary elevations and sections to most clearly describe it.

Further exploration revealed the motion of the cheese grater. Three distinct movements are involved in the function of the object: cutting, rotating, and tightening. Demonstrated through a series of drafts, these verbs came to life in the final drawing, illustrating each movement separately using various line weights and construction lines.

Using the final iteration of the combined sections, my partner and I began organizing the necessary pieces in order to construct a final model. This included revising the measurements and using compatible jointery to create a model that was both structurally sound and aesthetically pleasing. To accomplish this, a digital copy of the model was made with the proper measurements.

The wooden model represents the final iteration of our design. The angular extrusions passing through the band are connected using a variation of the dado joint, adding a seamless effect. This displays one of our primary design principles articulated throughout the project: the band is hidden, allowing the extrusions to appear airy and weightless.
